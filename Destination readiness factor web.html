Methodology

This tool is a thinking aid, not a decision algorithm. Its value lies in making the basis for judgment explicit and consistent across teams. The sections below explain the structural logic, scoring formula, weighting profiles, and known limitations.

Why two factors are gatekeepers

Security sits at the top of the dependency chain because it is the precondition under which all other factors become operationally meaningful. A destination may have shelter, food, and medical capacity — but if it is not safe to move civilians to or within it, none of those resources can be accessed. Security also enables consent and willingness, the two factors that then unlock resource access.

Local authorities consent is the administrative mechanism through which resources become accessible. Without it, evacuees may be turned back, humanitarian organisations may be unable to operate, and capacity, shelter, and food/water cannot be allocated to the arriving population.

In Profile A (the default), each gatekeeper carries double weighting. If either is set to Blocked, the score is capped at 20%. Other profiles vary this assumption — see "Weighting as perspective" below.

Readiness score: how it is calculated

Each assessed factor (any status other than Unknown) is assigned a score value and a weight from the active profile. Unknown factors are excluded from both numerator and denominator, so a partial assessment produces a valid score based only on what is known.

Score = Σ(value × weight) ÷ Σ(weight of assessed factors) × 100

Weighting as perspective

The same seven factors and the same factor statuses can produce different readiness scores depending on which priorities are encoded into the assessment. The tool offers three illustrative profiles — labelled as starting points, not finalised research outputs.

ProfileKey emphasisGatekeeper cap
A — Security-led Security + consent ×2; all others ×1 20% if security or consent blocked
B — Humanitarian access Consent ×2; security ×1.5; shelter, food & water, medical ×1.5 20% if consent blocked
C — Community-led Willingness ×2; security + shelter ×1.5; all others ×1 None — no single factor auto-overrides

These profiles are illustrative starting points rather than validated weighting schemes. A key open question is which axis of variation is most relevant: differences between assessing actors (military planners, humanitarian organisations, host communities), differences between geographic or political contexts, or differences between evacuation types. Each would suggest a different research design — for example, eliciting weights from practitioners representing different actor types, or testing whether a single profile produces sensible results across real-world case studies.

Known limitations

  • Profiles are hypothetical, not validated. The per-factor weights are illustrative and have not been elicited from practitioners or grounded in cited literature. They are open to challenge and revision.
  • Weighting is a design choice, not an empirical model. The double weighting and 20% cap in Profile A reflect operational logic, not validated field data.
  • Status inputs depend on field assessment quality. "Partial" and "Operational" require consistent interpretation. The tool creates a shared vocabulary but does not define precise thresholds — teams should establish working definitions for their context.
  • The dependency map is a simplification. The ten relationships shown are the most operationally significant, not an exhaustive set. The dependency structure is held constant across profiles in this version; a more advanced version might allow relationships themselves to vary by context.
  • The score is a prompt, not a threshold. No percentage value should be treated as a go/no-go decision point. It is designed to surface bottlenecks and structure conversation, not replace practitioner judgment.
